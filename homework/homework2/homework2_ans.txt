The parameter g represents the asymmetry parameter of the phase function, which controls the directional distribution of scattered light. Specifically, it is the mean cosine of the scattering angle, determining whether light tends to scatter forward, backward, or equally in all directions.
When g is positive, it indicates forward scattering, where light primarily continues in its original direction. As you increase g towards 1, the volume will appear significantly brighter when looking towards the light source, creating a strong halo or glow effect around the light, because the light is being directed forward toward the camera. When g is 0, scattering is isotropic, meaning light scatters equally in all directions, resulting in a uniform appearance. These changes occur because the g parameter fundamentally alters the probability density function of the scattering angles, concentrating the scattered light energy into specific directions relative to the incoming light.

Next event estimation is generally more efficient than phase function sampling when the scene contains small, intense light sources or when the medium is optically thin, as it explicitly connects scattering events to light sources rather than relying on random chance to hit them. Jim Kajiya famously has predicted in 1991 that "in 10 years, all rendering will be volume rendering". What do you think that makes him think so? Why hasn’t it happened yet?

Jim Kajiya likely made this prediction because volume rendering offers a unified theory of light transport that is physically more general than surface rendering. In the physical world, distinct surfaces are essentially mathematical abstractions; real matter is composed of particles with varying densities, meaning that the surface rendering equation is actually just a special, limiting case of the volume rendering equation where the density becomes infinite at a boundary. Kajiya foresaw that treating everything as a volume would eliminate the need for separate, ad-hoc algorithms for subsurface scattering, hair, fog, and solids, effectively unifying all rendering under one elegant mathematical model. However, this transition has not fully happened yet primarily because of computational cost and sampling efficiency. Treating hard, opaque surfaces as extremely dense volumes creates "stiff" mathematical problems where the mean free path of light is microscopic, requiring an impractical number of samples to resolve sharp edges without noise. Current boundary-representation methods remain significantly faster and more efficient for rendering opaque objects than simulating them as dense clouds of particles.

For heterogeneous volumes, what kind of distribution of the volume density makes the null scattering efficient/inefficient? Can you think of a way to improve our current sampling scheme in the inefficient case?


Null scattering becomes highly inefficient when the volume density distribution is sparse or highly variance-heavy, specifically where there are small, localized regions of extremely high density surrounded by large areas of low or zero density. In this scenario, the global majorant (maximum density) must be set to the highest peak to remain valid, which forces the tracking algorithm to take infinitesimally small steps even when traversing empty space, resulting in a massive number of wasted "null" collisions. Conversely, it is most efficient when the density is relatively uniform or consistently close to the global maximum, as the probability of a valid collision remains high. To improve the current sampling scheme in the inefficient case, we could implement a spatially adaptive structure such as a macro-grid, octree, or KD-tree that stores local majorant densities for different sub-regions of the volume. This would allow the sampler to "skip" empty space using large steps based on a low local majorant, while only switching to fine-grained stepping when the ray enters regions where the density is actually high.

How do we make the null-scattering work for emissive volumes? Briefly describe a solution.
To make null-scattering work for emissive volumes, we need to account for the light emitted by the medium along the ray path, not just the attenuation. A solution is to accumulate emission during the delta tracking loop. As the algorithm probabilistically steps through the volume (generating null or real collisions), we can treat these steps as Monte Carlo samples for the emission integral. At each step, we evaluate the volumetric emission at the current position, weight it by the current path transmittance (throughput), and add it to the accumulated radiance. This effectively integrates the source term along the ray while simultaneously sampling the distance to the next scattering event.
