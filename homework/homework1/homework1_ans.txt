Q1 Diffuse
Q1.1 Compare the two BRDFs fbaseDiffuse and fsubsurface with a Lambertian BRDF (try to render images with all three BRDFs): what differences do you see? Why?
Ans: The Lambertian BRDF acts as a perfectly matte baseline where radiance is independent of the viewing angle. Compared to Lambertian, the Disney Base Diffuse ($f_{baseDiffuse}$) exhibits view-dependent variance due to the Fresnel effect. On smooth surfaces, the edges appear darker (grazing darkening) because energy is diverted to the specular lobe (energy conservation). On rough surfaces, it simulates retro-reflection, resulting in a flatter appearance. The Disney Subsurface ($f_{subsurface}$) model appears significantly flatter and softer than the Lambertian model, resembling wax or skin. This is because it approximates the Lommel-Seeliger law, which simulates light penetrating the surface and scattering internally. Mathematically, the subsurface term counters the standard cosine falloff at grazing angles, preventing the edges from darkening as sharply as a standard diffuse surface.

Q1.2 Play with the roughness parameter: how does it affect the appearance?
Ans: Visual Differences: Increasing the subsurface parameter blends the material from the Base Diffuse model to the Subsurface model. The Subsurface model appears significantly "flatter" and softer than the Base Diffuse model. The shading gradient on the sphere looks more uniform, and the transition from light to shadow (the terminator) is much less harsh compared to the Base Diffuse model.

Why: The Base Diffuse model simulates surface reflection where light is scattered at the interface, following a Fresnel-modified cosine falloff. The Subsurface model, however, approximates the Lommel-Seeliger law, which simulates light penetrating the surface and scattering within the volume before exiting. Mathematically, the Subsurface formula contains a geometric term (inverse of the sum of cosines) that counteracts the standard cosine falloff. This causes the brightness to drop off much more slowly as the surface turns away from the light, creating the characteristic flat appearance of materials like wax or skin.

Lighting Condition: The difference is most noticeable under grazing lighting conditions (when the light source is positioned at a sharp angle relative to the surface normal, or near the silhouette of the object). In this condition, the Base Diffuse BRDF would typically become very dark due to the cosine law and energy conservation. In contrast, the Subsurface BRDF remains relatively bright because it simulates volumetric scattering allowing light to transport through the medium even at shallow angles.

Q1.4 Another popular option for modeling diffuse surfaces is the Oren-Nayar BRDF [9],
which is used in the Autodesk’s Standard Surface BSDF. What is the difference between the Oren-
Nayar BRDF and the Disney diffuse BRDF? What are the pros and cons? What is your preference?
Ans: Differences: The fundamental difference lies in their derivation approach. The Oren-Nayar model is a physically-based microfacet model derived theoretically assuming V-shaped cavities on the surface. It explicitly simulates how surface roughness causes retro-reflection and darkening. In contrast, the Disney Diffuse model is an empirical model designed to fit measured data from the MERL database. Instead of simulating micro-geometry, Disney simply modifies a Lambertian base using a Schlick Fresnel factor to artificially introduce grazing darkening for smooth surfaces and retro-reflection for rough surfaces.

Pros and Cons: The main advantage of the Disney Diffuse model is that it is computationally cheaper and simpler to implement than Oren-Nayar. It is highly production-friendly because its roughness parameter intuitively maps to visually plausible results that match real-world measured materials. Its main disadvantage is that it is not strictly physically based, being an ad-hoc fit. On the other hand, the Oren-Nayar model provides a more rigorous physical representation of rough diffuse surfaces like clay or sand. However, its disadvantage is that it is computationally more expensive involving complex trigonometric calculations and can be harder to importance sample efficiently compared to the cosine-weighted sampling used for Disney Diffuse.

Preference: I prefer the Disney Diffuse model. Its principled approach prioritizes artistic directability and efficiency over strict physical correctness. The fact that it uses a single roughness parameter to effectively transition between smooth appearances with dark edges and rough appearances with flat edges makes it much more versatile for general shading tasks in a path tracer. Oren-Nayar is often overkill unless rendering specific dry, rough materials.

Q2 Metal
Q2.1 Compare DisneyMetal with the roughplastic material (try to render images with both BRDFs). What differences do you see? Why?
Ans: Visual Differences: The most obvious difference is the color of the specular highlights. For DisneyMetal, the specular highlights are tinted by the material's base color. For RoughPlastic, the specular highlights remain white (achromatic) regardless of the base color, as the base color only appears in the diffuse component. Additionally, DisneyMetal appears much brighter and more reflective overall, especially at normal incidence (looking directly at the surface), whereas RoughPlastic has weak reflections at normal incidence and only becomes highly reflective at grazing angles.

Why: This is due to the fundamental physical difference between conductors (metals) and dielectrics (plastics). First, regarding the Fresnel Term, the DisneyMetal model approximates the Fresnel term using the base color as the reflectance at normal incidence. This means metals reflect a significant amount of light even when facing the viewer. In contrast, RoughPlastic models a dielectric interface where the reflectance at normal incidence is derived from the Index of Refraction (usually around 0.04), resulting in much dimmer reflections at face-on angles. Second, regarding Tinting, metals absorb light energy into their structure, which tints the reflected light. Dielectrics reflect light at the surface boundary without absorption, so the specular reflection matches the color of the light source, while the color of the plastic comes from subsurface scattering in the diffuse component.


Q2.2 Change the roughness parameters. Apart from how specular the surface is, do you observe any other differences?
Ans: As the roughness parameter increases, the most noticeable difference apart from the blurriness is the reduction in the peak intensity or brightness of the specular highlight. This happens because of energy conservation; as the reflected light is distributed over a wider solid angle, the energy density at the center of the highlight must drop to keep the total reflected energy constant. Furthermore, due to the specific parameterization in the Disney model where alpha is calculated as the square of the roughness, the visual transition appears perceptually linear. This means the material changes appearance at a steady, intuitive rate across the slider, rather than changing drastically at low values and barely changing at high values.

Q2.3 A popular alternative over the Trowbridge-Reitz normal distribution function is the Beckmann distribution (a Gaussian distribution on the slopes hlz/hlx and hlz/hly of the normals). What are the differences between Trowbridge-Reitz and Beckmann? Why did Disney folks choose to use Trowbridge-Reitz in-stead of Beckmann? (You might want to read the awesome article Slope Space in BRDF Theory from Nathan Reed.)
Ans: The primary difference between the two distributions is the shape of their tails, which dictates how the specular highlight fades out. The Beckmann distribution behaves like a Gaussian distribution in slope space, meaning the highlight intensity drops off very sharply and quickly as the angle moves away from the reflection vector. In contrast, the Trowbridge-Reitz distribution (also known as GGX) has much "heavier tails," meaning the intensity falls off much more slowly and persists further out from the center of the highlight. Disney chose to use Trowbridge-Reitz because their analysis of the MERL measured material data showed that real-world materials fit this heavy-tail behavior much better than the sharp cutoff of the Beckmann distribution. The heavy tails allow Trowbridge-Reitz to capture the soft, hazy halo often seen around the main highlight on real surfaces, which Beckmann fails to reproduce.

Q2.4 What are the pros and cons of the Schlick approximation compared to the actual Fresnel equation? What is your preference? (You may want to read/watch the Naty Hoffman presentation on Fresnel's equations.)
Ans: The main advantage of the Schlick approximation is that it is intuitive and artist-friendly. It allows the Fresnel effect to be controlled simply by the reflection color at normal incidence (F0), enabling artists to directly paint materials like gold or copper using RGB colors. In contrast, the actual Fresnel equations for conductors require a complex index of refraction (composed of n and k values) for every wavelength. This is a significant disadvantage because these physical constants are unintuitive for artists to tweak and are technically incorrect when applied directly to wide RGB color channels instead of specific wavelengths in a spectral renderer. While the actual Fresnel equation is physically exact, the Schlick approximation offers a much better balance of performance and usability for RGB rendering. I prefer the Schlick approximation for metals because, as noted by Naty Hoffman, using physical IOR values in an RGB renderer is often fraught with errors and usability issues, whereas Schlick provides predictable, high-quality visual results that are easy to control.

Q3 Clearcoat
Q3.1 Compare DisneyClearcoat with DisneyMetal using similar roughness. What differences do you see? Where do the differences come from?
Ans: Comparing DisneyClearcoat to DisneyMetal at similar roughness levels reveals two main differences. First, visually, the DisneyClearcoat highlight appears to have a much more prominent, soft halo or tail extending further outward from the main reflection spot compared to DisneyMetal. Second, the Clearcoat reflection is always achromatic (white or gray), whereas the Metal reflection is tinted by the material's base color. These differences stem from the underlying mathematical models and physical types. Physically, DisneyMetal models a conductor where light interacts with the material structure causing color absorption, while Clearcoat models a clear dielectric layer with a fixed index of refraction of 1.5, resulting in uncolored reflections. Mathematically, the differences come from the Normal Distribution Functions. DisneyMetal uses the Trowbridge-Reitz (GGX) distribution. However, Burley found that even GGX tails were not wide enough for certain materials, so DisneyClearcoat uses a specifically modified NDF designed to have significantly heavier tails to capture that specific look.

Q3.2 For coating, Autodesk Standard Surface uses a standard Trowbridge-Reitz microfacet distribution, instead of the modified normal distribution function proposed by Burley. What are the pros and cons? What is your preference?
Ans: The pros of the Autodesk approach (using standard Trowbridge-Reitz) are physical correctness and sampling efficiency. Standard Trowbridge-Reitz corresponds to a valid geometric microfacet configuration and supports advanced techniques like Visible Normal Sampling, which reduces noise. In contrast, the Disney clearcoat NDF is an ad-hoc fit with no clear geometric meaning , and it lacks a known method for visible normal sampling, making it mathematically less elegant and harder to importance sample efficiently. However, the pro of the Disney approach is that it successfully models the extremely long tails observed in measured data that standard Trowbridge-Reitz cannot capture. I prefer the Autodesk approach because sticking to physically valid distributions allows for more robust and unified sampling strategies across the renderer, whereas the Disney model introduces a special case that is computationally less elegant.

Q3.3 Why do Burley limit the clearcoat BRDF to be isotropic?
Ans: Burley limits the clearcoat BRDF to be isotropic because physically, a clearcoat represents a top layer of dielectric material (like varnish, lacquer, or car paint) applied over a base. Since these coatings are typically applied as fluids, surface tension causes them to self-level and distribute evenly, naturally eliminating any directional structure or grooves. Therefore, the resulting interface is almost always isotropic. Adding anisotropy to the clearcoat would be physically implausible for the vast majority of materials this lobe is designed to simulate, and it would unnecessarily complicate the model which is already an ad-hoc fit designed specifically to capture heavy specular tails.

Q4 Glass
Q4.1 Why do we take a square root of baseColor in the refractive case?
Ans: We take the square root of the baseColor because the baseColor parameter is designed to represent the final, perceived color of the light after it has passed through the entire object. Since a light ray typically interacts with the object's boundary twice to travel through it (once entering and once exiting), the color attenuation happens at both interfaces. Therefore, the BSDF at a single interface must use the square root of the color so that the cumulative effect of passing through two interfaces results in the intended color. 

Q4.2 Play with the index of refraction parameter η (the physically plausible range is (1,2]). How does it affect appearance?
Ans: Changing the index of refraction (eta) affects both the strength of the reflections and the amount of distortion seen through the object. As you increase the parameter towards 2, the object appears more "solid" and gem-like. This is because a higher index of refraction causes light to bend more strongly as it enters the surface (stronger refraction), resulting in greater distortion of the background. Simultaneously, it increases the Fresnel reflectance at normal incidence, making the surface appear much shinier and more reflective overall. Conversely, as you decrease the parameter towards 1, the object looks thinner and more "ghostly" like a soap bubble. At values very close to 1, the light barely bends, and the surface barely reflects, making the object almost invisible.

Q4.3 If a refractive object is not a closed object and we assign it to be a glass BSDF, will everything still work properly? Why? Propose a solution if it will not work properly (hint: you may want to read the thin-surface BSDF in Burley’s note [2].).
Ans: No, it will not work properly. The standard glass BSDF assumes the object is a closed volume with a well-defined interior and exterior. The physics logic relies on a ray entering the surface (refracting based on the IOR) and eventually hitting a back-face to exit (refracting back to the original medium). If the object is not closed (e.g., a single flat polygon), the ray enters the "glass" medium and bends, but never encounters an exit interface to bend back. This causes the renderer to treat the entire space behind the polygon as a solid block of glass, leading to incorrect, extreme distortions. The solution proposed by Burley is to use a "thin-surface" BSDF. This model approximates a very thin volume by simulating the interaction of both the front and back interfaces simultaneously. Crucially, it calculates the transmission and reflection intensities but does not refract (bend) the transmitted ray direction, allowing it to pass straight through like light through a thin window pane.

Q4.4 Replace the dielectric Fresnel equation with a Schlick approximation (see Burley’s course notes [2] on the fix to the Schlick approximation to make it work for η < 1). Do you observe any differences when η = 1.5? What about η = 1.01?
Ans: When eta is 1.5, there is very little observable difference between the Schlick approximation and the actual Fresnel equation. The approximation is generally accurate enough for standard glass materials that the visual result is almost identical. However, when eta is 1.01, the difference is drastic. As Burley noted, the Schlick approximation breaks down significantly when the index of refraction is close to 1. In this range, the Schlick approximation predicts reflections that are far too strong—up to 40 times brighter than reality. This causes the object to appear artificially visible with a distinct glowing outline, whereas with the actual Fresnel equation, an object with an index of 1.01 should be nearly invisible and ghost-like.

Q5 Sheen
Q5.1 Render the simple_sphere scene with the sheen BRDF. What do you see? Why? What happens if you change the position of the light source?
Ans: When rendering the simple_sphere scene with the sheen BRDF, I observe a soft, velvet-like glow concentrated at the edges of the sphere, rather than a sharp specular highlight in the center. This happens because the sheen model uses a modified Schlick Fresnel term to isolate and boost reflection specifically at grazing angles. This is intended to simulate the appearance of cloth or fuzz, where micro-fibers cause strong scattering at the silhouette. If I change the position of the light source, this "rim light" effect shifts to maintain its position at the grazing angles relative to the light and view vectors, emphasizing the retro-reflective nature of cloth materials where light is scattered back near the source direction.

Q5.2 Play with the parameter sheenTint, how does it affect the appearance? Why?
Ans: Increasing the sheenTint parameter changes the color of the grazing sheen reflection. When sheenTint is 0, the sheen reflection is purely white (achromatic), regardless of the material's underlying color. As you increase the parameter towards 1, the sheen color gradually shifts to match the hue and saturation of the base color. This happens because the mathematical model interpolates between a white value and a normalized tint value derived from the base color. This control is necessary because different fabrics behave differently in the real world: some have transparent or white fuzz (requiring low sheenTint), while others have dyed fibers where the retro-reflection carries the color of the material itself (requiring high sheenTint).

Q5.3 In Autodesk Standard Surface, the sheen is modeled by a microfacet BRDF [4]. What are the pros and cons between the Autodesk approach and the Disney approach? What is your preference?
Ans: The main advantage of the Autodesk approach is physical plausibility. By modeling sheen as a microfacet BRDF (often simulating cylindrical fibers), it correctly accounts for masking, shadowing, and energy conservation. This results in a more realistic representation of how light interacts with the complex mesostructure of cloth. Its disadvantage is higher computational complexity and potentially more difficult importance sampling compared to a simple analytical formula. In contrast, the Disney approach is an ad-hoc modification of the Schlick Fresnel term. Its pros are extreme computational efficiency and simplicity; it is very easy to implement and cheap to evaluate. Its con is that it lacks physical grounding; it is essentially a hack to brighten the edges and does not model the actual scattering geometry of fibers. My preference leans towards the Autodesk approach for high-end rendering because accurate cloth rendering often relies on the subtle physical interactions that a mere Fresnel boost cannot fully capture. However, for real-time applications or background assets, the Disney approach is superior due to its performance.

Q6.1 What are the differences between the specular and metallic parameters? How do they affect the appearance?
Ans: The metallic parameter determines the fundamental physical nature of the material, blending it between a dielectric (insulator like plastic or wood) and a conductor (metal like gold or iron). Visually, increasing the metallic parameter removes the diffuse component and tints the specular reflections with the base color, giving the characteristic look of a metal. In contrast, the specular parameter controls the intensity of the specular reflection specifically for the dielectric (non-metallic) component. It essentially acts as an artistic control for the index of refraction, allowing you to make a plastic surface look shinier or duller. Increasing the specular parameter makes the highlights on a non-metallic surface brighter without changing their color or removing the underlying diffuse base.

Q6.2 What are the differences between the roughness and clearcoat_gloss parameters? How do they affect the appearance?
Ans: The roughness parameter controls the microstructure of the base material layers (diffuse, metal, and glass). Visually, increasing roughness makes the primary reflection spread out and become blurrier. In contrast, the clearcoat_gloss parameter specifically controls the smoothness of the additional clearcoat layer that sits on top of the base. The most important difference is that their behaviors are inverted mappings. While increasing the standard roughness parameter makes the surface rougher (higher alpha), increasing the clearcoat_gloss parameter makes the clearcoat layer smoother (lower alpha). Therefore, a high clearcoat_gloss value results in a very sharp, tight reflection on the coating, while a high roughness value results in a dull, blurred reflection on the base.

Q6.3 Play with the specularTint parameter. How does it affect the appearance?
Ans: The specularTint parameter affects the color of the specular highlights specifically for the non-metallic (dielectric) component of the material. By default, when specularTint is set to 0, the specular reflections on a dielectric surface are achromatic (white or gray), which mimics the physical behavior of most common materials like plastic where the reflection does not absorb the material's color. As you increase the specularTint parameter towards 1, the color of these highlights shifts to match the hue and saturation of the material's base color. This allows artists to simulate complex materials or stylistic effects where the specular reflection appears to pick up the color of the surface beneath it, rather than just reflecting the white light source.

Q6.4 The roughness parameter affects many components of the BSDFs at once (e.g., both the diffuse and metal BRDF use the roughenss parameter). How do you feel about this? If you are an artist using the Disney BSDF, would you want to have a separate roughness parameter for each component?
Ans: I feel that sharing the roughness parameter is a smart design decision that aligns perfectly with the goal of being "principled" and artist-friendly. Physically, roughness describes the geometric microstructure of the surface. Since this microstructure is a property of the object itself, it logically influences all light interactions—whether it is the retro-reflection in the diffuse lobe or the spread of the highlight in the metal lobe. Therefore, linking them ensures that the material remains physically consistent and plausible without requiring the artist to manually match two separate sliders. If I were an artist, I would generally not want separate parameters. Having a single roughness control (and requiring only a single roughness texture map) significantly streamlines the workflow and reduces the chance of creating "broken" materials where the diffuse and specular responses disagree on the surface geometry. While separate controls might technically offer more freedom, the efficiency and consistency gained by the unified parameter are far more valuable for standard production work.
